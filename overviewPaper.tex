In 1958, Herbert Riehl and Joanne Malkus (citeRiehl) first showed that the large scale circulation in the tropics and thus its poleward energy export can only be explained when including the effect of small scales. 
Deep convective clouds, on scales on the order of a few kilometers, maintain the equatorial heat budget, a conclusion they derived using mean equatorial cross sections constructed from radiosondes launched within the equatorial trough. 
These results critically shaped the GATE campaign in 1974, the largest atmospheric field campaign of all times citeZhang, which had, as one of its two primary objectives the goal "To estimate the effects of smaller-scale tropical weather systems on the large-scale circulations" citeKuettner. 
%A key reason for this interest was the need to better parameterize the effect of tropical convection in large-scale numerical models.
The results of GATE showed, however, that the scale interaction is more difficult and that the large scale circulation cannot simply be explained by the sum of individual convective clouds but that the organisation of clouds into mesoscale patterns has to be accounted for citeHouze. 
Despite these results, a clear scale separation between small and large scales is still commonly assumed, despite mounting evidence that mesoscale processes have to be accounted for citeShaw, citeKlocke. 

It is with this focus on scale interactions, with a particular focus on the mesoscale, that the ORCESTRA campaign citeStevens set out to improve our understanding of the Intertropical Convergence Zone. 
In August and September 2024, 
In the Intertropical Convergence Zone the air ascends and exports energy and humidity polewards. 
This large scale ascending motion is inseparable from the cloud formation on small scales since the rising motion happens within the clouds.
It has, therefore, long been recognized that there is a link between the large scale circulation and the cloud scale circulations. 
Clouds within the ITCZ are, however, not randomly distributed but organize into a number of mesoscale patterns (or no clouds at all) citeFrank. 
This simple statement has been known since ever since the first satellite observations.
In recent years, this organization has received increased attention citeBony as model and observational studies suggest that the degree of organisation impacts key properties such as the amount of radiative cooling (citeBretherton, citeTobin), the efficiency with which energy is exported (citeMasunaga), or extremes in precipitation (citeBao). 
Motivated by this, the PERCUSION subcampaign of ORCESTRA statistically sampled vertical profiles of clouds, aerosols, humidity, and mesoscale vertical motion in and at the edge of the Atlantic ITCZ.   

PERCUSION, importantly, contributed to the overall campaign effort by frequent coordination with the other platforms involved in ORCESTRA, see citeStevens, and the full potential of the data collected during the campaign will only be done justice by combining measurements across subcampaigns. For example, where PERCUSION saw the clouds from above--the research vessel based ship subcampaign BOWTIE (citeKlocke) saw the clouds from below and with it the ocean properties beneath it. Within PERCUSION particularly critical was the coordination with the EarthCARE satellite. A key goal of PERCUSION was to contribute to the calibration and validation of the satellite launched just in time before the campaign. To this end, the flights in the tropics were supported by additional flights in the extratropics. 
The reader is referred to citeGroß for a description and the results of this central aspect of the campaign.
The goal of the paper is to describe the data collected during PERCUSION as well as the scientific motivation behind our data collection strategy. 



\textbf{Transition regions.} On the timescale of days, the deep precipitating tropics are well-defined by a sharp gradient in column water vapor in the meridional direction citeMapes2018. Moist regions and dry regions, separated by a column water vapor threshold of 48mm citeMapes or 50mm citeMasunaga. Crossing this threshold the amount of convection picks up, the surface winds converge citeJulia, and convection more often than not is intensified at the edge citeMasunaga.  
In addition to the meridional transition regions, important vertical transition regions exist as well, e.g. in the Atlantic for the Saharan Air Layer carrying dust across the Atlantic, modifying clouds and radiative cooling on their way citeGutleben \todo{Need to understand Gutleben study better}.

\textbf{Doldrums.} Inside the ITCZ... Surface winds are key for coupling the ocean to the atmosphere--the stronger the surface winds the larger the surface fluxes and the moister and warmer the atmosphere. It has long been known (though until recently somewhat forgotten citeKlocke) that the region of the ITCZ is also the region of frequently occurring low wind speed events, the so called doldrums. The doldrums are persistent and large enough to being able to strand sailing ships. While traditionally thought to be linked to the region of ascending motion, we recently showed that this does not fit with buoy based observations that the doldrums tend to be free of precipitation citeWindmiller. This has led to the hypothesis that the air within the doldrums is subsiding. This shows that we don't know the first thing about the doldrums, even though they are important for the spatial distribution of convection, at least in models citeSegura, and can impact ocean heat waves citeRichards.

\textbf{Moist static energy budget--dynamics perspective.} One key property of the ITCZ is to export energy. How much energy is exported does not only matter for the extra-tropics citeHou1998 but the efficiency of which also impacts the properties of the Hadley cell (citeKang, citeByrne). To calculate the energy export, the vertical structure of both the dynamic and thermodynamic atmospheric state is key. In short only a deep overturning circulation  exports energy. In reanalysis, the vertical motion profile, and thus energy export out of the ITCZ has been shown to depend on latitude (citeBack). 
The energy export has also been shown to depend on the mesoscale structure of the ITCZ, i.e. whether or not the ITCZ is edge intensified, (citeMasunaga). Idealised simulations even suggest that there are mechanisms that if, left undisturbed, lead to the formation of clusters of deep convection with a mesoscale circulation which leads to an upgradient transport of moist static energy and thus revert the expected energy export out of regions of deep convection (citeBretherton)--energy is imported rather than exported.

\textbf{Moist static energy budget--radiation perspective.} On a global scale, the difference between the incoming and outgoing radiation is referred to as earth's energy imbalance--the fundamental driver of climate change. Earth's energy imbalance has increased much faster than expected over the last two decades citeMauritsen. The radiation budget, i.e. how much of the incoming solar radiation is reflected or emitted back to space, depends on the vertical structure of clouds, humidity, aerosols, as well as the surface properties. All of these components interact: detrained cloud condensate evaporates and increases the humidity, aerosols impact cloud droplet formation and properties, humid layers can help maintain aerosol layers citeGutleben which, in turn impact the radiative heating profile. Need observations to test our process understanding citeBrett.

%What we don't know
Despite it's apparent importance, our knowledge about what the mesoscale and vertical structure of the deep tropics structure actually looks like is severely limited. This is particularly true for vertical motion where most of our understanding is based on modeling or reanalyses results even though we know from the few comparisons we have that their accuracy highly depends on the situation (citeHuaman, citeGeorge). The latter results are partially based on recent field campaigns (EUREC4A, OTREC). It is also true for the mesoscale, and in particular for how the mesoscale interacts with the small and large scale, because until recently climate models were unable to capture this. 

This paper describes PERCUSION the German research aircraft based subcampaign of ORCESTRA (citeStevens). ORCESTRA the international field campaign in the tropical Atlantic took place in August and September 2024. 
With a total of seven platforms, extensive measurements were collected on board of a research vessel, two island based observatories, three research aircrafts, and the EarthCARE satellite. While the latter is, strictly speaking, not part of the campaign its calibration and validation played such a crucial role in coordinating the different subcampaigns and its data will be invaluable to analyse the data collected during the campaign and putting them into context that it deserves specific mentioning.

PERCUSION took place  with help of the German research aircraft HALO in its cloud observatory configuration (citeStevens2019). During PERCUSION a vertically resolved thermodynamic (temperature and water vapor), dynamic (large-scale winds and vertical velocity), and particulate (hydrometeors, clouds, and aerosols) state of the atmosphere was collected over the area of flight operations. This was made possible by HALO's cloud observatory configuration, which combines active and passive remote sensing instruments with in-situ measurements from the nose-boom at flight level and vertical profiles from dropsondes. 

This is particularly true for PERCUSION since the subcampaign itself united two related but yet distinct goals. While PERCUSION's contribution to ORCESTRA will be the focus of this paper, PERCUSION also played a critical role in the calibration and validation of the EarthCARE satellite. This part of PERCUSION which also includes a set of research flights performed with the same configuration but out of Germany extending the measurements collected during PERCUSION to polar regions is described in a separate paper (citeGroß).
