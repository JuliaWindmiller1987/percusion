In 1958, Riehl and Malkus transformed our understanding of the tropical atmosphere. While it was known that the large-scale overturning circulation exports energy from the tropical upper troposphere towards the poles, the mechanism by which energy is transported from the surface to the upper troposphere remained unclear. \citet{riehlHeatBalanceEquatorial1958} showed that this vertical transport can be achieved by just a few thousand deep convective clouds, carrying the released heat almost undiluted into the upper troposphere, at any given time, revealing that the large-scale circulation cannot be understood without storm-scale processes.
This insight helped to motivate the GATE campaign of 1974, the largest atmospheric field study to date \citep{zhangRelocationGATEPacific2022}, which aimed 


GATE already revealed that individual convective clouds alone cannot explain the circulation and that mesoscale organisation is important \citep{houzejr.ConvectionGATE1981}, yet the importance of mesoscale processes is still often neglected \citep{shawOtherClimateCrisis2025}. With the help of large domain or global storm-resolving models \citep{satohGlobalCloudResolvingModels2019}, the role of mesoscale processes and their interaction with storms and larger-scale phenomena can now be assessed more directly. Benchmarking these models and testing the hypotheses derived from them requires targeted observations on these scales. In response to this need, the ORCESTRA campaign returned to the GATE region after 50 years of global warming.

% PERCUSION overview
The ORCESTRA campaign took place from August to September 2024 (Stevens et al., under review). Measurements were taken across the Atlantic, from east to west, with a focus on the Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) and the surrounding subsiding regions. Observations were collected from the ocean surface and to the top of the atmosphere using three research aircraft, a research vessel and two observatories, as well measurements coordinated with the new EarthCARE satellite. Of the eight sub-campaigns, this paper summarises PERCUSION, a campaign centred on the German research aircraft HALO. Configured as a cloud observatory, HALO's high-altitude and long-range capabilities allowed it to provide "snapshots" of the mesoscale structure and vertical motion field of the ITCZ \citep{bonyMeasuringAreaAveragedVertical2019, georgeJOANNEJointDropsonde2021}. Its instrumentation was designed to resemble that of EarthCARE, enabling PERCUSION to make a significant contribution to the satellite’s calibration and validation (Groß et al., under review).

Sampling focused on both the edges and the interior of the ITCZ. Recent work shows that the ITCZ exhibits sharp, dynamically meaningful edges, even on an hourly timescale. These edges coincide with steep gradients in column water vapour (CWV) of 48–50 mm \citep{Mapes2018, masunagaMechanismMaintenanceSharp2020}, separating the moist deep tropics from the subsiding region. As CWV can be reliably observed from space, PERCUSION used the CWV field to inform its sampling strategy. Framing observations in CWV space also connects naturally to idealised studies of tropical convective organisation (Wing; Mueller), the relevance of which to the real-world ITCZ remains uncertain \citep{Bony2015}.

% Mesotopics
Sampling the clouds and mesoscale structure of the ITCZ in CWV space was driven by a set of specific questions. While not exhaustive, the following “mesotopics” offer a brief introduction to the core research themes.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Moist static energy budget.} The tropical large-scale circulation exports moist static energy poleward, influencing not only the extratropics \citep{trenberthSeamlessPolewardAtmospheric2003} but also the latitude, width, and strength of the ITCZ \citep{kangTropicalResponseExtratropical2009, byrneNarrowingITCZWarming2016}. Although this export is typically considered on long temporal and spatial scales, its connection to convection and mesoscale organisation remains poorly understood. Regional variations in export efficiency \citep{backGeographicVariabilityExport2006} and dependencies on mesoscale convective patterns \citep{masunagaEdgeIntensificationEastern2023} point to links with zonal and meridional differences in cloud properties. Idealised studies further show that the vertical and horizontal structure of moisture--and its coupling with clouds and radiation--can even lead to an import of energy into convective regions \citep{Bretherton2005}.
    \item \textit{Doldrums.} Mesoscale regions with near-zero surface winds, known as doldrums, occur most frequently within the ITCZ \citep{Klocke2017a, windmillerCalmVariableInner2024}. As surface winds regulate the exchange of heat and moisture between the ocean and the atmosphere, such windless conditions can enhance marine heatwaves \citep{richardsMeteorologicalDriversMass2024} and suppress convection in kilometre-scale models \citep{seguraSingleTropicalRainbelt2025}. However, the traditional explanation for doldrums does not apply over short time scales \citep{windmillerCalmVariableInner2024}, leaving several fundamental questions unanswered: What is their vertical and horizontal structure? What processes lead to their formation and maintenance? How do they modify surface fluxes? And do the doldrums ultimately import or export moist static energy?
    \item \textit{Transition regions.} Precipitation often peaks at the edges of the ITCZ rather than at its centre, which has important implications for the vertical structure of clouds, radiative heating and, according to reanalysis data, vertical motion \citep{masunagaEdgeIntensificationEastern2023}, and likely aerosols as well. However, the processes that maintain the sharp moisture gradients at these edges \citep{Mapes2018, masunagaMechanismMaintenanceSharp2020} and determine when convection intensifies there remain unclear \todo{Rephrase to account for the process suggested by Hiro}. Do cold pools help determine the width of the ITCZ, as suggested by idealised simulations \citep{Nolan2016, windmillerConvectionEdge2019}? And how do aerosols vary across the ITCZ edges, influencing and being influenced by the vertical structure of humidity \citep{gutlebenImpactsWaterVapor2019}?
\end{itemize}

% Synthesis
Together, these mesotopics highlight a shared challenge: the key processes that shape the ITCZ operate on spatial and temporal scales that are not well observed. Progress requires high-resolution vertical profiles of clouds, moisture and winds — data that is difficult to obtain from satellites and is therefore inadequately represented in reanalyses \citep{Huaman2022}. By sampling the ITCZ in CWV space and resolving its interior, edges, and surrounding subsidence region, PERCUSION provides the multiscale, vertically resolved observations needed to address these questions. The outline of the paper is as follows. Section 2 provides a detailed description of the measurement strategy and the data collected. Section 3 offers an initial overview of the data and demonstrates its usefulness in addressing the above questions, before concluding in Section 4.
