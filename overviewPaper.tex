\section{Introduction}

In 1974, the Global Atmospheric Research Program (GARP) Atlantic Tropical Experiment (GATE)--the largest atmospheric field study to date \citep{zhangRelocationGATEPacific2022}--set out to ``explore the mechanism by which solar heat stored in the tropical oceans drives the global circulation of the atmosphere, and to incorporate this mechanism into numerical models'' \citep{kuettnerGeneralDescriptionCentral1974}. 
A key motivation was the recognition that deep convective clouds--fundamental to the tropical circulation \citet{riehlHeatBalanceEquatorial1958}--``slip through the mesh'' of the synoptic observing network \citep{kuettnerGeneralDescriptionCentral1974}.
To collect observations to analyse the effect of deep convective clouds, or smaller weather-systems, on the large-scale circulation was, therefore, one of the primary objectives. 
The results showed that convective and large-scale processes are not well separated but rather that mesoscale processes need to be accounted for \citep{houzejr.ConvectionGATE1981}.
Despite these early insights, the assumption that small and large spatial scales can be cleanly separated has remained fundamental to climate science. 
While this framework has successfully captured many aspects of anthropogenic climate change, increasing regional discrepancies between observed and simulated changes have renewed attention on mesoscale processes and their coupling to both small- and large-scale processes \citep{shawOtherClimateCrisis2025}.

With a particular focus on the mesoscale, the ORCESTRA (Organized Convection and EarthCARE Studies over the Tropical Atlantic) campaign \textit{cite{Stevens et al., in review}} returned to the tropical Atlantic in 2024, exactly fifty years after GATE. 
Taking place from August to September 2024, the campaign combined three research aircraft, a research vessel and two observatories, as well as coordinated measurements with the EarthCARE satellite, launched less than three months before the start of the campaing. 
The close link between HALO and EarthCARE measurements will also help generalise the results from the campaign period and study region to other times and locations—a common challenge for targeted field campaigns.
Together, these platforms sampled the Atlantic Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) and adjacent subsiding regions from the surface to the lower stratosphere. 

Of the eight sub-campaigns, this paper summarises PERCUSION, a campaign centred on the German research aircraft HALO. Configured as a cloud observatory, HALO's high-altitude and long-range capabilities allowed it to provide "snapshots" of the mesoscale structure and vertical motion field of the ITCZ \citep{bonyMeasuringAreaAveragedVertical2019, georgeJOANNEJointDropsonde2021}.  HALO's instrumentation was designed to resemble that of EarthCARE.
The calibration and validation of EarthCARE was a central component of campaign and is described by \textit{Groß et al. (in review)}. 

The scientific questions at the heart of the PERCUSION campaign centre on how strongly the “inner life” of the Atlantic ITCZ \citep{windmillerInnerLifeAtlantic2024} shapes its outer life. 
More specifically, we examine how mesoscale patterns of clouds, precipitation, surface winds, humidity, and aerosols impact the radiative balance and circulation in the deep tropics.

The deep tropics play an important role in the global circulation system as they mark the region of mean ascending motion of the Hadley cell.
While it has long been recognized that the circulation exports energy to the extra tropics, the efficiency of this export is both highly uncertain and key for predictions of key properties of the tropical rainbelts.
An important source of uncertainty is that this efficiency is closely tied to the profile of vertical motion of the atmosphere in the deep tropics which has been suggested to depend on the structure of the ITCZ. 
For example when the ITCZ is in an edge- vs. non-edge-intensified state or in cases when the inside of the ITCZ is marked by weak surface winds--typical for the doldrums. 
To constrain vertical motion in and at the edges of the ITCZ, and to relate it to the structure of the ITCZ, was a key objective of the PERCUSION campaign.
%The vertical motion of updrafts depends on latent heating, which in term depends on the vertical transport of humidity and entrainment of environmental air (this also sets the precipitation efficiency). 

The deep convection in the tropics not only plays a central role in the overturning circulation but is also crucial for the Earth's radiation budget. 
The deep tropics are marked by a large cloud cover and high humidity. 
Tropical high clouds are a key contribution to our uncertainty in climate simulations. 
Their impact on the radiation budget depends on the microphysical properties of the clouds (for example ice cloud particle size distribution) and their macro-physical properties (for example their frequency, height and anvil size and thickness). 
The latter properties have been shown to depend on their mesoscale organisation. 
Not only the clouds but also the humidity in tropics has been shown to correlate with mesoscale organisation: the more aggregated the clouds the more outgoing longwave radiation. 
These analyses where motivated by idealised model simulations. 
While the relationship between mesoscale patterns and radiation has, therefore, been found, important questions regarding what drives this organisation remain. 
For example, what role do cloud radiative heating and horizontal gradients in heating due to differences in humidity profiles play in organising convection and concentrating atmospheric humidity?
In the tropical Atlantic, especially the East Atlantic, radiation is also impacted by the high aerosol load due to the Saharan air layer. 
Again, it has been shown that vertical profiles of radiation--impacted by the presence of aerosols and humidity--are key for their maintenance. 
How these aerosol driven changes in heating rates impact cloud formation (and thus in turn the radiation budget) in this area, remains an open question.
To analyse the importance of mesoscale organisation of clouds and the radiation budget, PERCUSION focused on tropical high clouds, developing retrievals to constrain their ice water path and ice droplet size distributions, as well as the vertical distribution of humidity and aerosol. 

Aggregation of convection into increasingly large spatial scales can, at least as shown in idealised simulations and known under the name of self-aggregation can be driven by a number of different mechanisms, even in the complete absence of any externally driving mechanism, it remains unclear which--if any--of these mechanisms lead to clustering of convection in the real atmosphere. One key difference between the suggested mechanisms is if they act locally, i.e. through a local amplification of humidity anomalies, or if radiatively driven up-gradient transport of humidity is key. 
One factor that has been shown to strongly modulate the tendency to self-aggregate is entrainment. 
The data collected during PERCUSION aims to answer these questions by collecting data in and at the edges of the ITCZ and by contrasting entrainment rates with different states of organisation. 
ITCZ as large self-aggregation cluster...

One reason why self-aggregation has attracted a lot of attention in the last years is because of its potentially strong impact on key atmospheric properties. 
Aggregation of convection not only impacts the spatial distribution of precipitation but also precipitation extremes. 
Changes in cloud organisation furthermore impact cloud properties and environmental humidity thus having strong impact on radiation. 
In particular, an increase in aggregation has been linked to and thus a decrease in environmental humidity--leading to an increase in outgoing longwave radiation and a decrease in high clouds----leading to an increase in incoming short wave radiation (An enhanced organization of deep convection is associated with a drier troposphere, fewer high clouds, and a radiative cooling of the tropics citeBony2020). 
Questions addressed by PERCUSION are, therefore, how cloud radiative properties depend on mesoscale organisation. The radiative feedback of tropical high clouds is, more generally, are a key uncertainty in climate sensitivity. Questions addressed by PERCUSION therefore also include the question of how macroscopic (cloud size and extent) compared to microscopic cloud properties (ice particle size distribution and total ice amount) impact the cloud-radiation interaction.  
PERCUSION will also contribute to algorithm retrieval for ice particles and ice water path.

Another cloud pattern that is, in addition to clustering, in the focus of PERCUSION is convection intensified at the edges of the ITCZ. The edge of the ITCZ is marked by a sharp gradient in CWV. When considering the region of peak precipitation, precipitation peaks more often at either of the edges rather than in the centre. 
The properties of convection peaking at the edge are fundamentally different to the ones at the center, e.g. the overturning circulation associated with convection at the edge imports energy whereas the convection in the center exports energy. 
This is akin to precipitation peaking at the edge in more idelised setups. 
Different mechanisms driving edge-intensification have been suggested but require testing. 
In particular, measurements of vertical velocity profiles are necessary to determine the overturning circulation.

While convection has been shown to frequently peak at the edge of the ITCZ, extended regions of low surface winds are often found between these edges. 
These regions have been known for centuries but recently scientific interest has again increased in them. 
On the one hand, wind speeds are critical in coupling the atmosphere to the ocean--with almost vanishing winds leading to a decoupling. The resulting heating of the ocean has been associated with marine heat waves and coral bleaching whereas the cooling of the boundary layer suppresses convection, at least in storm resolving climate models.
On the other hand, recent work has shown that the traditional explanation of the doldrums cannot hold. Rather than marking a region of ascending air, the doldrums have been suggested to be marked by subsiding air. This requires testing. 

The winds associated with the Atlantic ITCZ, albeit at higher levels, are also key in transporting 50 to 250 Mt of Saharan mineral dust towards the Caribbean each year in an elevated layer known as the Saharan air layer. 
The high aerosol load within the Sahran air layer impacts the radiation budget and can serve as a nucleus for cloud formation. 
The dust and, has been shown more recently, the humidity of the Saharan air layer interacts with radiation in a way that can both help to maintain the elevated layer itself in a way sensitive to the vertical structure of both.

To summarise, the scientific objectives of PERCUSION center around the moist static energy budget of the deep tropics. Both from a dynamic point of view: what role does horizontal and vertical transport of moist static energy play for organising convection and how does that feed back on the larger scale circulation? But also from a radiation point of view: how does radiative heating and cooling from humidity, clouds, and aerosols impact clouds and their organisation as well as the radiation budget? Specific regions of interest are the edges and the inner life of the ITCZ.  For the edges: what sharpens the gradient between moist and dry regions and what causes precipitation to frequently peak there and how does it interact with the Saharan air layer? 
For the inner life, what shapes its dynamic and thermodynamic profiles on short time scales, especially when the surface winds disappear? 

% Sampling strategy 

To address these questions PERCUSION sampled the ITCZ first in the eastern part of the tropical Atlantic (near Sal) and then in the western part of the tropical Atlantic (east of Barbados). 
Measurements were collected in a statistic and unbiased manner--rather than e.g. targeting specific cloud systems in the ITCZ--the flight plans followed, as far as possible, a generic pattern. 
The generic pattern was: our flight paths were oriented back and forth along the EarthCARE track. 
This not only allowed to collect targeted data for the calibration and validation during the time of the actual overpass (overpass a couple of seconds but direct comparison possible for about plus minus twenty minutes) but also gives some time evolution as we collect three vertically resolved cross-sections of the ITCZ (flights back and forth plus satellite measurements). 
Along the entire flight track, vertical profiles of clouds, humidity, aerosols, as well as... were collected. 
To sample the edges and center of the ITCZ, column water was used to identify the northern and southern edges of the ITCZ rather than a fixed spatial coordinate. 
Intensified measurements, in particular vertical velocity measurements from dropsonde circles, were conducted at these edges and in between the edges. 
As will be described in the paper, it turned out to be easier to follow this generic pattern in the eastern part of the Atlantic. The outline of the paper is as follows.
