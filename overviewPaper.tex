\section{Introduction}

 % MSE budget (radiation and dynamic), doldrums, transition regions.

In 1958, Herbert Riehl and Joanne Malkus (citeRiehl) first showed that the large scale circulation in the tropics and thus its poleward energy export can only be explained when including the effect of small scales. 
Deep convective clouds, on scales on the order of a few kilometers, maintain the equatorial heat budget, a conclusion they derived using mean equatorial cross sections constructed from radiosondes launched within the equatorial trough. 
These results critically shaped the GATE campaign in 1974, the largest atmospheric field campaign of all times citeZhang, which had, as one of its two primary objectives the goal "To estimate the effects of smaller-scale tropical weather systems on the large-scale circulations" citeKuettner. 
%A key reason for this interest was the need to better parameterize the effect of tropical convection in large-scale numerical models.
The results of GATE showed, however, that the scale interaction is more difficult and that the large scale circulation cannot simply be explained by the sum of individual convective clouds but that the organisation of clouds into mesoscale patterns has to be accounted for citeHouze. 
Despite these results, a clear scale separation between small and large scales is still commonly assumed, despite mounting evidence that mesoscale processes have to be accounted for citeShaw, citeKlocke. 

It is with a focus on scale interactions in general, but with a particular focus on the mesoscale, that the ORCESTRA campaign citeStevens set out to improve our understanding of the Intertropical Convergence Zone. 
In August and September 2024, three research aircrafts, one research vessel, two observatories, and through intense coordination with the new EarthCARE satellite, we sampled the tropical convergence zone and the surrounding subsiding region, spanning the entire Atlantic from east to west and from the sea floor through the atmosphere. 
%The full potential of the data collected during the campaign will only be done justice by combining measurements across subcampaigns. For example, where the research aircrafts saw the clouds from above or within--the research vessel based ship subcampaign saw the clouds from below and with it the entire ocean properties beneath it. 
This paper summarizes PERCUSION--the subcampaign centred around the German research aircraft HALO in its cloud observation configuration citeStevens.  
The goal of the paper is to give an overview of the scientific motivation for PERCUSION, the measurement strategy and the data collected in the deep tropics.
In addition to PERCUSION's contribution to ORCESTRA, there is also PERCUSION's contribution to EarthCARE calibration and validation. These two aspects are invariably linked and yet serve distinct purposes that the latter is discussed separately in citeGroß.

% This large scale ascending motion is inseparable from the cloud formation on small scales since the rising motion happens within the clouds.
% It has, therefore, long been recognized that there is a link between the large scale circulation and the cloud scale circulations. 
% Clouds within the ITCZ are, however, not randomly distributed but organize into a number of mesoscale patterns (or no clouds at all) citeFrank. 
% This simple statement has been known since ever since the first satellite observations.
% In recent years, this organization has received increased attention citeBony as model and observational studies suggest that the degree of organisation impacts key properties such as the amount of radiative cooling (citeBretherton, citeTobin), the efficiency with which energy is exported (citeMasunaga), or extremes in precipitation (citeBao). 
% Motivated by this, the PERCUSION subcampaign of ORCESTRA statistically sampled vertical profiles of clouds, aerosols, humidity, and mesoscale vertical motion in and at the edge of the Atlantic ITCZ.   

In line with the overarching goals of the ORCESTRA campaign, a key motivation of the PERCUSION campaign was to better understand what shapes the mesoscale structure of the ITCZ. 
What distinguishes convection intensified at the edges from convection intensified in the center citeJulia, citeMasunaga? and what happens inside the ITCZ when the winds disappear citeKlocke, citeStevens, citeWindmiller? 
To answer these questions, we first needed to define the ITCZ on short time scales. 
%On weekly or monthly time scales, the ITCZ exhibits a seasonal cycle that can be seen in, for example, the region of pronounced near surface convergence (citeBerry, citeWodzicki), region of frequently occuring deep convection (citeWaliser), or highest precipitation rates (citeSchneider). 
This is not trivial since, the ITCZ latitude can shifts significantly on these time scales--identifying the latitude of the ITCZ based on the region of highest convective activity, citeFrank found variations in latitude of up to $7\deg$ in two days. 
More recently, citeMapes2018 showed that, using satellite snapshots, the edge of the ITCZ is well-defined by a region of high column water vapour, with a column water vapor threshold of 48mm citeMapes or 50mm citeMasunaga. Crossing this threshold one enters a region of intense rainfall, the surface winds converge citeJulia, and convection more often than not is intensified at one of the edges rather than in the centre citeMasunaga. 
Identifying the ITCZ in CWV space also readily connects to studies that have shown the impact of the organization of tropical convection simulated under highly idealized conditions (see citeWing for a review). These studies have shown that under homogeneous boundary and initial conditions, different mechanisms can help the aggregation of convection with important consequences for the atmospheric mean state. To test the importance of these mechanisms under more realistic conditions remains an outstanding challenge citeBony.

%\textbf{Doldrums.} Inside the ITCZ... Surface winds are key for coupling the ocean to the atmosphere--the stronger the surface winds the larger the surface fluxes and the moister and warmer the atmosphere. It has long been known (though until recently somewhat forgotten citeKlocke) that the region of the ITCZ is also the region of frequently occurring low wind speed events, the so called doldrums. The doldrums are persistent and large enough to being able to strand sailing ships. While traditionally thought to be linked to the region of ascending motion, we recently showed that this does not fit with buoy based observations that the doldrums tend to be free of precipitation citeWindmiller. This has led to the hypothesis that the air within the doldrums is subsiding. This shows that we don't know the first thing about the doldrums, even though they are important for the spatial distribution of convection, at least in models citeSegura, and can impact ocean heat waves citeRichards. 
%Missing: a vertically resolved picture of the doldrums, including vertical velocity clouds, ...

All three, edge vs. centrally itensified convection, the doldrums, and convective self-aggregation have been suggested to impact the vertical velocity profile and thus the energy export out of the ITCZ or convective region. To export energy polewards is one of the key features of the ITCZ and depends on the vertical structure of both the dynamic and thermodynamic atmospheric state. How much energy is exported does not only matter for the extra-tropics (e.g. citeHou1998) but the efficiency of which also impacts the properties of the Hadley cell (citeKang, citeByrne). 
Despite its importance it is unclear what sets this efficiency, a question that is hampered severely by our limited observational reference profiles of vertical motion. While many studies are based on modeling or reanalyses results even though we know from the few comparisons we have that their accuracy highly depends on the situation as shown by measurements from recent field campaigns (citeHuaman, citeGeorge).

In addition to the vertical motion and thus dynamic perspective of the tropical energy budget, another important component is the energy input and loss due to radiation. On a global scale, the difference between the incoming and outgoing radiation is referred to as earth's energy imbalance--the fundamental driver of climate change. Earth's energy imbalance has increased much faster than expected over the last two decades citeMauritsen. The radiation budget, i.e. how much of the incoming solar radiation is reflected or emitted back to space, depends on the vertical structure of clouds, humidity, aerosols, as well as the surface properties. All of these components interact: detrained cloud condensate evaporates and increases the humidity, aerosols impact cloud droplet formation and properties, humid layers can help maintain aerosol layers citeGutleben which, in turn impact the radiative heating profile. Need observations to test our process understanding citeBrett.

The discussion above can be summarised into four guiding mesotopics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item How does the organization of clouds on the mesoscale impact the energy export?
    \item How does the interaction between radiation, clouds, humidity and aerosols shape the energy gain and loss of the atmosphere?
    \item How are the edges, or transition regions, of the ITCZ maintained?
    \item How are windstill regions inside the ITCZ maintained?
\end{itemize}

To address the questions above, in particular, and to complement our understanding about the structure of the ITCZ, in general, we used the German research aircraft HALO to collect data of the ITCZ in CWV space. The data collected includes (but is not limited to) vertically resolved thermodynamic (temperature and water vapor), dynamic (large-scale winds and vertical velocity), and particulate (hydrometeors, clouds, and aerosols). A detailed description of our measurement strategy and data collected will be given in section 2. To give a first glimpse at the data collected and to illustrate their usefulness in addressing the questions raised above, we illustrate the usefulness or our collected data in an examplary fashin with respect to the questions above in section 3 before concluding in section 4.

\section{Conclusion}

PERCUSION was a measurement campaign with very high resolution data in space and time. 
Disadvantage: limited to a single region and season but our close link to EarthCARE allows us to, in some degree, overcome the disadvantage of an intensive measurement period extrapolate our findings to different regions and seasons.
