\section{Introduction}

% ORCESTRA follows in the footsteps of GATE but shifts its focus to the mesoscale
In 1958, Herbert Riehl and Joanne Malkus (Riehl) were the first to demonstrate that the large-scale circulation in the tropics, spanning distances of 1000 km or more, could not be explained without considering the impact of clouds on scales of 10 km or less.
This finding was pivotal in shaping the GATE campaign, which took place in the tropical Atlantic in the summer of 1974. The largest atmospheric field campaign to date, GATE, aimed to estimate the impact of smaller-scale tropical weather systems on large-scale circulation in order to improve its representation in global circulation models. 
However, the results from GATE already showed that interactions between scales in the tropics are more complex, and that the large-scale circulation cannot be explained solely by the combined effect of individual convective clouds. Rather, the organisation of clouds into mesoscale patterns must be considered, as demonstrated by Houze, although this is still often neglected today (Shaw). 
Now, with the new generation of global storm-resolving models (Hohenegger), assessing the role of mesoscale processes and their interaction with storms and large-scale phenomena has become more tangible. The need to benchmark these models, and more importantly the need to test hypotheses based on these models, has increased the demand for observations focusing on these scales. To contribute to these efforts, the ORCESTRA campaign (Stevens) returned to the GATE campaign region after 50 years of global warming (Stevens). 

% PERCUSION is one of the subcampaigns of ORCESTRA--particularily suited to investigate the mesoscale structure of the ITCZ and the related vertical velocity field
The ORCESTRA campaign took place in August and September 2024. Measurements were concentrated in the Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) and the surrounding subsiding region. Measurements were taken across the entire Atlantic, from east to west, intensifying those west of Cabo Verde and east of Barbados. Measurements were also taken from the seafloor through the entire atmosphere. This was achieved with the help of three research aircraft, one research vessel and two observatories, as well as through intense coordination with the new EarthCARE satellite. A total of eight sub-campaigns were conducted (campaign papers). 
This paper summarises the PERCUSION sub-campaign, centred around the German research aircraft HALO. 
Throughout the campaign, HALO was configured as a cloud observatory (Stevens). Thanks to its ability to fly at high altitudes and cover long distances in a short time, it was uniquely positioned to capture 'snapshots' of the mesoscale structure and vertical motion profiles of the ITCZ (Glöckner) and the clouds within. At the same time, the cloud observatory configuration of the HALO aircraft was designed to resemble the instrumentation of the EarthCARE satellite, which was launched just in time for the campaign. Thus, PERCUSION also played a critical role in calibrating and validating EarthCARE, as described by Groß. 

% During PERCUSION the ITCZ edges and inner life were of key interest and defined using CWV
When sampling the ITCZ, we put a particular emphasize on measuring the edge as well as the interior of the ITCZ. That the ITCZ has, even on short time scales, meaningfully defined edges and an inner life has recently been demonstrated by Mapes and Windmiller. While otherwise often collapsed into a single line (Waliser... ) or defined as a region of high mean precipitation rates or extended regions of low level convergence, definitions which only become meaningful when averaging over a weeks or months, Windmiller showed that on hourly time scales, that sharp edges of the ITCZ exist and that they are marked by rapid changes in the meridional wind speed and direction. These edges  align with the margins of the moist deep tropics, separted from the subsiding region by a steep gradient in column water vapour, CWV, (Mapes), with a threshold of either 48 mm (Mapes) or 50 mm (Masunaga). As CWV is more easily observed from space, PERCUSION used the CWV field to direct its measurements. Collected data in a way that can be thought of as being collected based on CWV space had the further advantage that it connects to studies of the organisation of tropical convection under highly idealised conditions (see Wing and Mueller for reviews) whose impact is still a key uncertainty (Bony). 

% 
In line with the overarching goals of the ORCESTRA campaign, a key motivation of the PERCUSION campaign was to improve our understanding of the factors shaping the mesoscale structure of the ITCZ. 
What distinguishes convection intensified at the edges from convection intensified in the centre, Julia and Masunaga, and what happens inside the ITCZ when--in the doldrums--the surface winds disappear, Klocke, Stevens and Windmiller? 

Edge vs. centrally intensified convection, the doldrums and convective self-aggregation have all been suggested to impact the vertical velocity profile, and thus the energy exported out of the ITCZ or convective region. Exporting energy polewards is one of the ITCZ's key features and depends on the vertical structure of the atmospheric state, both dynamically and thermodynamically. The energy exported is important not only for the extra-tropics (e.g. citeHou1998), but the efficiency of that export also impacts the properties of the Hadley cell (citeKang, citeByrne). 
Despite its importance, it is unclear what determines this efficiency — a question severely hampered by our limited observational reference data on vertical motion. Many studies are based on modelling or reanalysis results, even though we know from the few comparisons we have that their accuracy depends heavily on the situation, as shown by measurements from recent field campaigns (Huaman, George).

In addition to vertical motion, which provides a dynamic perspective on the tropical energy budget, another important component is energy input and loss due to radiation. On a global scale, the difference between incoming and outgoing radiation is referred to as the Earth's energy imbalance—the fundamental driver of climate change. Earth's energy imbalance has increased much faster than expected over the last two decades (cite Mauritsen). The radiation budget — i.e. how much of the incoming solar radiation is reflected or emitted back into space — depends on the vertical structure of clouds, humidity and aerosols, as well as surface properties. All of these components interact: for example, detrained cloud condensate increases humidity, aerosols impact cloud droplet formation and properties, and humid layers can maintain aerosol layers (Gutleben), which in turn impact the radiative heating profile. We need observations to test our process understanding (McKim).

The above discussion can be summarised into four guiding mesotopics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item How does the organisation of clouds on the mesoscale impact the energy export?
    \item How does the interaction between radiation, clouds, humidity and aerosols shape the energy gain and loss of the atmosphere?
    \item How are the edges, or transition regions, of the ITCZ maintained?
    \item How are windstill regions inside the ITCZ maintained?
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

PERCUSION was a measurement campaign with very high resolution data in space and time. 
Disadvantage: limited to a single region and season but our close link to EarthCARE allows us to, in some degree, overcome the disadvantage of an intensive measurement period extrapolate our findings to different regions and seasons.
