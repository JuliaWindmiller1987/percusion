\section{Introduction}

In 1974, the Global Atmospheric Research Program (GARP) Atlantic Tropical Experiment (GATE)--the largest atmospheric field study to date \citep{zhangRelocationGATEPacific2022}--set out to ``explore the mechanism by which solar heat stored in the tropical oceans drives the global circulation of the atmosphere, and to incorporate this mechanism into numerical models'' \citep{kuettnerGeneralDescriptionCentral1974}. 
A key motivation was the recognition that deep convective clouds--fundamental to the tropical circulation \citet{riehlHeatBalanceEquatorial1958}--``slip through the mesh'' of the synoptic observing network \citep{kuettnerGeneralDescriptionCentral1974}.
To collect observations to analyse the effect of deep convective clouds, or smaller weather-systems, on the large-scale circulation was, therefore, one of the primary objectives. 
The results showed that convective and large-scale processes are not well separated but rather that mesoscale processes need to be accounted for \citep{houzejr.ConvectionGATE1981}.
Despite these early insights, the assumption that small and large spatial scales can be cleanly separated has remained fundamental to climate science. 
While this framework has successfully captured many aspects of anthropogenic climate change, increasing regional discrepancies between observed and simulated changes have renewed attention on mesoscale processes and their coupling to both small- and large-scale processes \citep{shawOtherClimateCrisis2025}.

With a particular focus on the mesoscale, the ORCESTRA (Organized Convection and EarthCARE Studies over the Tropical Atlantic) campaign \textit{cite{Stevens et al., in review}} returned to the tropical Atlantic in 2024, exactly fifty years after GATE. 
Taking place from August to September 2024, the campaign combined three research aircraft, a research vessel and two observatories, as well as coordinated measurements with the EarthCARE satellite, launched less than three months before the start of the campaing. 
The close link between HALO and EarthCARE measurements will also help generalise the results from the campaign period and study region to other times and locations—a common challenge for targeted field campaigns.
Together, these platforms sampled the Atlantic Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) and adjacent subsiding regions from the surface to the lower stratosphere. 

Of the eight sub-campaigns, this paper summarises PERCUSION, a campaign centred on the German research aircraft HALO. Configured as a cloud observatory, HALO's high-altitude and long-range capabilities allowed it to provide "snapshots" of the mesoscale structure and vertical motion field of the ITCZ \citep{bonyMeasuringAreaAveragedVertical2019, georgeJOANNEJointDropsonde2021}.  HALO's instrumentation was designed to resemble that of EarthCARE.
The calibration and validation of EarthCARE was a central component of campaign and is described by \textit{Groß et al. (in review)}. 

The scientific questions at the heart of the PERCUSION campaign centre on how strongly the “inner life” of the Atlantic ITCZ \citep{windmillerInnerLifeAtlantic2024} shapes its outer life. 
More specifically, we examine how mesoscale patterns of clouds, precipitation, surface winds, humidity, and aerosols impact the radiative balance and circulation in the deep tropics.

The deep tropics play an important role in the global circulation system, as they mark the rising branch of the Hadley Cell. It has long been known that this rising branch transports energy from the deep tropics towards the subtropics, but the efficiency of this transport, which controls central properties of tropical rain belts, remains uncertain. This limits our ability to predict how they change in a changing climate. The efficiency with which the deep tropics export energy depends on the vertical structure of the circulation--in particular, the profile of vertical motion--as well as the thermodynamic structure of the atmosphere. These properties of the atmosphere are largely determined by the properties of the clouds within.
 
While properties of clouds are influenced by small-scale processes such as convective entrainment, which modulates the buoyancy of plumes, detrainment, and the vertical distribution of moisture, clouds are also influenced by their mesoscale organisation. 
With respect to the inner life, or meridional structure of the ITCZ, recent studies suggest that the location where clouds form relative to the edge of the deep tropics fundamentally alters the ascent within these clouds and therefore the energy export. 
In cases of edge-intensified convection, the vertical motion within the deep tropics may be further influenced by the presence of the doldrums, i.e. extended regions of weak surface winds, between the edges. While the doldrums have long been considered the footprint of the ascending motion within the ITCZ, recent work suggests that they are instead associated with subsidence. 
Together, these findings suggest an interaction between the Atlantic ITCZ's meridional structure, the clouds within it, and the efficiency with which it exports energy. 

To quantify these interactions, PERCUSION measured vertical structure of the circulation, thermodynamic structure of the atmosphere, entrainment, meridional structure of the ITCZ, edge-intensified convection, doldrums

Not only do the deep tropics play a central role in the global circulation, but their extensive cloud cover and high humidity also make them crucial to the Earth's radiation balance. Tropical high clouds are the focus of intensive research, as their radiative feedback significantly impacts the accuracy of climate projections. These clouds' influence on radiation depends on both their microphysical properties, such as the size of ice particles, and their macrophysical properties, including frequency, height, size, and thickness. Research has shown that the macrophysical properties of high clouds depend on their mesoscale organisation. For instance, stronger aggregated convection is associated with fewer high clouds. Similarly, the moisture field in the tropics has been shown to correlate with mesoscale organisation: stronger aggregated convection tends to be associated with increased outgoing longwave radiation.
While these relationships between mesoscale organisation and radiation are well documented, important questions remain regarding the mechanisms that drive this organisation. Idealised simulations suggest that differential warming between clouds and cloud-free areas can promote convective organisation. However, assessing the relevance of these mechanisms under more realistic conditions remains a major challenge.
In the tropical Atlantic, particularly in the eastern basin, the radiation balance is influenced by the high aerosol load associated with the Saharan air layer. The increased amount of aerosols and moisture in this air layer alter the vertical profiles of radiative heating, which plays an important role in maintaining it. The question of how these changes in radiative heating rates influence cloud formation, and thus affect the regional radiation balance, remains unanswered.

% Key objectives
To constrain vertical motion in and at the edges of the ITCZ, and to relate it to the structure of the ITCZ, was a key objective of the PERCUSION campaign. 
To analyse the importance of mesoscale organisation of clouds and the radiation budget, 
PERCUSION focused on tropical high clouds, developing retrievals to constrain their ice water path and ice droplet size distributions, as well as the vertical distribution of humidity and aerosol. 

To summarise, the scientific objectives of PERCUSION center around the moist static energy budget of the deep tropics. Both from a dynamic point of view: what role does horizontal and vertical transport of moist static energy play for organising convection and how does that feed back on the larger scale circulation? But also from a radiation point of view: how does radiative heating and cooling from humidity, clouds, and aerosols impact clouds and their organisation as well as the radiation budget? Specific regions of interest are the edges and the inner life of the ITCZ.  For the edges: what sharpens the gradient between moist and dry regions and what causes precipitation to frequently peak there and how does it interact with the Saharan air layer? 
For the inner life, what shapes its dynamic and thermodynamic profiles on short time scales, especially when the surface winds disappear? 

% Sampling strategy 

To address these questions PERCUSION sampled the ITCZ first in the eastern part of the tropical Atlantic (near Sal) and then in the western part of the tropical Atlantic (east of Barbados). 
Measurements were collected in a statistic and unbiased manner--rather than e.g. targeting specific cloud systems in the ITCZ--the flight plans followed, as far as possible, a generic pattern. 
The generic pattern was: our flight paths were oriented back and forth along the EarthCARE track. 
This not only allowed to collect targeted data for the calibration and validation during the time of the actual overpass (overpass a couple of seconds but direct comparison possible for about plus minus twenty minutes) but also gives some time evolution as we collect three vertically resolved cross-sections of the ITCZ (flights back and forth plus satellite measurements). 
Along the entire flight track, vertical profiles of clouds, humidity, aerosols, as well as... were collected. 
To sample the edges and center of the ITCZ, column water was used to identify the northern and southern edges of the ITCZ rather than a fixed spatial coordinate. 
Intensified measurements, in particular vertical velocity measurements from dropsonde circles, were conducted at these edges and in between the edges. 
As will be described in the paper, it turned out to be easier to follow this generic pattern in the eastern part of the Atlantic. The outline of the paper is as follows. Section 2 provides a detailed description of the measurement strategy and the data collected. Section 3 offers an initial overview of the data and demonstrates its usefulness in addressing the above questions, before concluding in Section 4.
